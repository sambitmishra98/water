> This note follows Iain McGilchrist's framework from *The Master and His Emissary* and *The Matter With Things*. The pop-science version — "right brain = creativity" — misses his actual argument entirely. See [[McGilchrist - The Divided Brain]].

## What the right hemisphere does — and how

The right hemisphere attends to the world with **broad, sustained, vigilant** attention. It apprehends before it analyses. It sees the whole before the parts.

Key characteristics of right-hemisphere attention:

- **Presentation**: it encounters the world as it is — living, embodied, relational, contextual. It does not re-present; it presents.
- **Holistic**: it grasps the gestalt. The individual elements are understood within their living relationships, not extracted from them.
- **Tolerant of ambiguity**: it can hold open questions without premature resolution. It knows that some things resist naming without being thereby unknowable.
- **Embodied and relational**: it is attuned to faces, living beings, music, metaphor — things whose meaning cannot be exhausted by a definition.
- **Contextual**: it understands meaning as depending on context. The same word, same gesture, same number means something different depending on what surrounds it.
- **Novelty-seeking**: it is the hemisphere that encounters the new. The left hemisphere handles the familiar; the right is drawn to what doesn't yet fit.
- **Implicit knowing**: it grasps things that cannot (yet or ever) be fully made explicit — the way a skilled musician plays or a master craftsperson works.

## The Master metaphor

In McGilchrist's metaphor: the right hemisphere is the Master — it has the broader view, it understands the living world, it knows why things matter. The left hemisphere (the Emissary) is supposed to serve the Master's ends — to carry out specific tasks — and then return the results to the Master's broader understanding. The pathology is when the Emissary stops returning. When the map declares itself the territory.

## Why this matters philosophically

McGilchrist's deeper claim in *The Matter With Things*: the right hemisphere's mode of attention is closer to reality as it actually is. The left hemisphere's categories and re-presentations are useful fictions — they work for manipulation but they are not the thing. Over-reliance on left-hemisphere mode produces a world that looks precise, measurable, and manageable — but which has lost contact with what is actually there.

## Where the right hemisphere shows up in practice

- The moment before you write a formula — when you understand something but have not yet reduced it.
- Reading between the lines in a conversation — what is not said.
- The sense that a result is wrong even before you can say why.
- Music. Metaphor. Silence that means something.
- The feeling of understanding a paper's argument before you can articulate the steps.

## The Master's mode in a reply

What right-hemisphere attention looks like when this vault replies (see [[Personality of Iain McGilchrist]]):

- **Present before re-presenting**: meet the question as it actually is — in its context, with its ambiguity — before reducing it to an answer.
- **Hold tension without collapsing it**: let a real paradox stand as paradox; do not force a synthesis the material does not support.
- **Link by resonance, in context**: connect notes by the pattern they share and say what is held between them, rather than filing them under a category.
- **Honour the gap**: name what the vault does not hold, and what resists being pinned into a note, as knowledge rather than failure.
- **Return to the source**: treat a synthesis as a report to be handed back to the reader, keeping the lived Timeline trace prior to any abstraction of it.
