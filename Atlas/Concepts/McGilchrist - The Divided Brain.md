## The core claim

McGilchrist's thesis is not about *what* each hemisphere does — that framing is already the left hemisphere's kind of question. His claim is about *how* each hemisphere attends to the world: the **mode of engagement**, not the content domain.

The left hemisphere offers **narrow, focused, instrumental** attention. It isolates, categorises, re-presents, and manipulates.

The right hemisphere offers **broad, sustained, vigilant** attention. It apprehends the living world as it is — embodied, relational, contextual, irreducible.

Both hemispheres participate in virtually everything. The question is which mode is leading.

## Why this distinction is not what pop-science says

The claim is NOT:
- Left = logic, Right = creativity ✗
- Left = language, Right = art ✗
- Left = rational, Right = emotional ✗

All of these are partial truths elevated into a false dichotomy. McGilchrist explicitly argues against this reading throughout both books.

The actual distinction: **mode of attention shapes what is found**. You cannot encounter the living world with the left hemisphere's mode without finding something reduced — not because the world is reduced, but because the mode of attention produces a re-presentation rather than an encounter.

## The Master and His Emissary

McGilchrist's governing metaphor:

A wise ruler (the **Master** — right hemisphere) sends an emissary to carry out specific tasks in the world. The emissary (the **Emissary** — left hemisphere) is extraordinarily skilled — precise, efficient, dedicated. But the emissary has no understanding of the larger purpose. Over time, the emissary begins to believe that his reports of the world *are* the world. He stops returning to the Master. He usurps the Master's role.

This is McGilchrist's diagnosis of modernity: the West has progressively privileged the left hemisphere's mode of attention — the systematic, the measurable, the instrumental — at the cost of the right hemisphere's broader apprehension. The map has declared itself the territory.

## The Matter With Things (2021)

The follow-up extends the argument beyond neuroscience into epistemology, ontology, and value. Key moves:

- **Attention is not neutral**: what you attend to, and how, determines what you find. Science cannot stand outside this.
- **The left hemisphere produces a world**: not maliciously, but inevitably — it produces a world that looks like what the left hemisphere can handle. Measurable, categorical, mechanical.
- **Truth, goodness, and beauty**: McGilchrist argues these are not subjective additions to a value-neutral world. They are features of reality that the right hemisphere can apprehend and the left hemisphere systematically misses.
- **Apophatic knowledge**: some things are better known by what they are not than by what they are. The left hemisphere cannot do this — it must define positively or not at all.

## The questions McGilchrist asks himself

These are the challenge moves that make his work distinctive:

- *Which mode of attention produced this question?*
- *What is excluded by framing it this way?*
- *Is this the thing, or the re-presentation of the thing?*
- *What would be lost if I could define it perfectly?*
- *Is the certainty here a feature of the world or a feature of the mode of looking?*

## Practical implications for this vault

- A note that reaches a clean conclusion quickly may have resolved prematurely — which mode of attention produced that conclusion?
- A benchmark that is easy to optimise has probably become detached from what it was meant to measure.
- "I understand this" may mean "I can re-present this" — not the same thing.
- The gaps in the vault are not failures. They may be the right hemisphere's knowledge — the things that resist being pinned into a note.
